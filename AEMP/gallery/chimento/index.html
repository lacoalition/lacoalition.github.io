Chimento Contemporary

Eva Chimento opened her Chimento Contemporary in 2015, leasing a studio space inside a large warehouse made up of different studio and residential spaces on Anderson Street in Boyle Heights. Despite her claims of being inclusive, Eva Chimento failed to create a space that served local residents. 

 

In the summer of 2016, Chimento attended a community call-in event with various organizations and other gallerists. Instead of listening to residents who were ready to explain how her gallery and others like it were impacting their lives, Chimento lashed out with self-righteous resentment about the use of the word “gentrifier.” At this 2016 community gathering at the Pico Gardens public housing site, she gave a paternalistic speech claiming that the community did not know how to spot actual enemies of gentrification. Also, by citing her one-time art workshop with youth of color, she asserted that her minimal contribution granted her unlimited access to do what she wanted in this gentrifying and exploited neighborhood.

 

In August 2018, after several call-outs and campaigns demanding galleries leave, Chimento announced that she was relocating her gallery and subleasing her space in Boyle Heights. At that time, she made a haphazard effort at offering her space to Defend Boyle Heights. She offered the coalition the remaining months on her lease, but indicated she would charge the community the full rent to occupy her space. The community rebuked her offer when they saw it merely as a means to only appear in solidarity while still getting paid for the space she was leasing as a gentrifier.  

 

Shortly after, Chimento Contemporary relocated to West Adams—a historically Black neighborhood also gentrifying—and was met with another demonstration against her business. During the rally, a gallery attendant called the police and the FBI on protesters and residents. Similar to other gallerists and gentrifiers, she had no concern for what police are capable of doing to community residents and her gentry entitlement put protesters directly at risk of authoritarian violence. 

 

Today, the Boyle Heights warehouse that used to house Chimento Contemporary continues to rent to high-priced hipster spaces, including a media studio and a coffee shop
