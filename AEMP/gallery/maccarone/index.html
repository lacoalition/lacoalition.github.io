Maccarone

A veteran gentrifier, Michelle Maccarone opened her first New York art gallery on the border between Chinatown and the Lower East Side in 2001.  At this time, these working-class neighborhoods were beginning to experience the effects of artwashing and gentrification. Her gallery was described by Vice as “a rickety building on Canal Street in Chinatown, back when nobody had galleries down there.” According to Vice, “The gallery quickly gained a reputation for being ‘edgy’ and ‘notorious’ and all those words you use to describe great, exciting stuff that you don’t get to see in a typical white cube in Chelsea.” As a member of the aspiring elite, this tactic of making use of working-class culture for social and literal capital, and its impacts on the lives of its neighbors—eviction and displacement—are minor concerns. “I was the first asshole who opened on the Lower East Side,” Maccarone jokes in her Vice interview, “I take complete responsibility for boug-ing up that neighborhood.”

 

In September 2015, the New York Times published a lengthy article on Macarone’s new space in Los Angeles: “New Art Galleries Enjoy a Los Angeles Advantage: Space.” Of Boyle Heights, Maccarone said, “It still has a dangerous quality—I kind of like that. I like that we spent a fortune on security.” This article is pivotal in a couple ways, the person writing it is oblivious to the geography of LA, conflating Downtown LA, Boyle Heights, and the Arts District. The article puts forward an idea that there is a large open frontier of abandoned property in this amorphous downtown area just waiting to be developed. This jumped out to locals as totally off the mark. In many ways, Boyle Heights is spatially dense, and the area the New York Times Real Estate section writers tagged as desolate, where Maccarone leased it’s 50,000 sq. ft, space, is located blocks from the Pico Aliso public housing projects. 

In September 2015, Maccarone opened its giant 50,000 sq. ft. location in a renovated warehouse down the street from 356 Mission in Boyle Heights. Maccarone rents her luxurious Boyle Heights space and has a 15-year lease. She borrowed the money for a renovation of the LA space against the value of her West Village gallery. According to a blog post by Kenny Schacter from September 2019, Maccarone is currently in financial trouble after being sued by two artists she failed to pay. Having moved from Chinatown in New York to Greenwich Village, Maccarone followed in the footsteps of many other New York galleries that opened second locations in Los Angeles. In the article “New Gallery to Inject a Bit of New York Into Downtown Art World of Los Angeles,” Maccarone describes how she got the idea for the location of her space from Laura Owens’s 356 Mission—itself a joint venture with Gavin Brown. Brown and Maccarone’s New York galleries were across the street from one another (although Brown has since moved on to Harlem). For decades, these galleries have moved from one working-class POC neighborhood to another in New York; to them, Boyle Heights is just a fresh vein of edginess and “danger” to mine for social capital.
