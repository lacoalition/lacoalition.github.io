IBID Gallery

Swedish gallerist Magnus Edensvard began his crusade to gentrify poor communities 10 years before he came to Los Angeles. In 2004, he opened his first gallery in what Art Territory then called “the once very cheap—but now very gentrified, and therefore no longer quite exciting—Hoxton Square” in east London. Edensvard’s outlook and approach to opening up a gallery was rooted in a racist mindset where wealthy artists could take advantage of properties for sale in disinvested neighborhoods and also contribute to the phenomenon of objectifying working class communities. 

 

In 2014 — Edensvard turned his sights on Los Angeles. He opened the Ibid Project in a warehouse in South Santa Fe in the Arts District and began touting Los Angeles as a paradise for art investors and real estate speculators. In an interview with Art Space, he called himself “an involuntary ambassador for the DTLA property scene”. By 2015, his efforts had succeeded perhaps too well: His own gallery received a 30-day notice to vacate.But Edensvard, unlike thousands of L.A. tenants facing eviction from their homes, especially in an ongoing pandemic, was able to negotiate with his landlord (not incidentally, a Beverly Hills-based investment firm focused on “emerging neighorboods”— or, gentrifying communities) for more time at the Arts District space.

In 2016, Ibid relocated across the river in Boyle Heights, taking up a space designed by Kulapat Yantrasat of trendy architecture firm wHY. 

 

IBID Gallery shared their art gallery warehouse space with another million-dollar gallery operation, UTA Artist Space. Members of BHAAAD and Defend Boyle Heights and Pico Gardens residents protested their galleries on a Saturday in July 2017 only to have the IBID Gallery staff call LAPD officers to their location in order to intimidate neighbors, residents, and community organizers. IBID Gallery, much like MaRS and Chimento and many more galleries, are easily frightened by women and people of color from Boyle Heights and disregarded the community’s safety by inviting trigger-happy law enforcement to a non-violent picket line. 

 

In the summer of 2018, IBID Gallery closed its doors in Boyle Heights and instead returned to the comfort of a wealthier and whiter neighborhood in West Hollywood.
