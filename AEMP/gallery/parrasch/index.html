Parrasch Heijnen Gallery

Franklin Parrasch has been dealing art since 1986. Having run six galleries in New York, Parrasch now has a gallery on the tony Upper East Side, a block away from Central Park. In January 2016, following the call of the art market to “go west,” he partnered with Christopher Heijnen to open a gallery in Boyle Heights on Boyle Avenue in a formerly industrial area. The program was purportedly guided by a “conscious reflection upon the process of creativity as related to human evolution,” while ignoring the human conditions of housing, artwashing, and gentrification that affect the neighborhood. 

 

Their 5,000 sq. ft. space used to be the site of a dinette and lunchroom for former Sears employees, who likely lived in and around Boyle Heights. The gallery space is now owned by Wallingford Investors Limited, based in Beverly Hills. The gallery’s proximity to the vacant Sears building is no doubt an intentional move by Parrash and Heijnen to situate themselves next to what will become creative office spaces for hipsters, techies, and gentrifiers with no intention of contributing to the existing community. The Sears building was once a workplace for many Boyle Heights, East LA, and Southeast LA residents. It is now owned by luxury real estate developer (and outspoken Republican) Izek Shomof. This real estate developer has built out work havens and high-end apartments for gentrifiers in different parts of Los Angeles, including the gentrifying and vulnerable landscapes of Chinatown, Inglewood, and Downtown LA. Shomof shared his vision in a disgusting video of what he wants the Sears building to become soo. Artists and gallerists, like Parrasch and Heijnen, will certainly embrace this type of pretentious and ostentatious luxury real estate.   


Parrasch and Heijnen knew exactly what they were doing when they moved to the Boyle Avenue warehouse and are likely cheering on the luxury development planned by multimillionaires like Shomof. It is not a coincidence that it’s also right next to the LA River, which is being touted by real estate agents who see its slick expansion as a selling point. More galleries will likely be enticed to open in this industrial part of Boyle Heights once the Sears building is redeveloped, and this will threaten the long-time tenure of working-class families nearby in Wyvernwood and Estrada Courts, as well as houseless residents along the local streets in this area. The presence of Parrasch Heijnen Gallery remains an open invitation to other real estate developers and gentrifiers to displace our neighbors and long-time businesses.
