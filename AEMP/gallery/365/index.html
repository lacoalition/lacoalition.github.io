356 Mission/Ooga Booga

Laura Owens is a painter based in Los Angeles represented by the New York–based blue-chip gallerist Gavin Brown. In 2013, bankrolled by Brown, Owens turned her 12,000-square-foot studio in Boyle Heights into the 356 Mission gallery. Many have called 356 Mission a “pioneer” of art spaces in Boyle Heights; its presence encouraged other galleries like Maccarone and Venus Over Los Angeles to open nearby.

 

Brown rented 356 Mission from Vera Campbell, a major property owner and gentrification machine responsible for artwashing developments across Los Angeles. Brown himself owns millions of dollars in real estate in New York City, and in 2016, jumped on the gentrification wave engulfing Harlem by relocating his flagship location from the West Village to a multistory building on West 127th St. 

 

In fall 2016, activists from Defend Boyle Heights and BHAAAD called a boycott of Boyle Heights galleries and began picketing events and openings in the neighborhood. In response, artist Ambar Navarro cancelled a scheduled screening at the gallery in February 2017; noiseheads, punks, and artists stopped Oxen Records from holding a show there in June 2017; and Chris Kraus was pressured to cancel her book launch in fall 2017. Defend Boyle Heights and BHAAAD also picketed and disrupted the opening of Owens’ solo show at the Whitney Museum in New York with support from local antigentrification groups and organizers. Dr. Nizan Shaked set a principled example for her colleagues by refusing to cross the picket line, and resigned from the board of X-TRA journal in solidarity with Boyle Heights in 2018. Even Owens’s former interns and unpaid staff revolted against the gallery’s exploitation of their free labor and chose to act in solidarity with immigrants, working-class families, and the hood. Finally, due to the militant, passionate, and unrelenting refusal from the mothers, workers, and comrades in Boyle Heights, with solidarity from people in the art world, the gallery closed its doors in spring 2018. 

 

It is unsurprising that high-intensity gentrifiers like Owens and Wendy Yao (who ran a branch of her Chinatown-based bookstore Ooga Booga inside 356 Mission) are still taking to the media to brazenly deny their role in gentrification and artwashing—all because they consider themselves “liberal” and full of “love.” But empty declarations of love don’t come close to cancelling out the terror and grief that displacement unleashes upon immigrants and working-class communities. 


Owens and Brown have the financial means to locate ANYWHERE in Los Angeles, in New York, or around the world. But they consistently choose to take advantage of low-income POC communities like Boyle Heights, Chinatown, and Harlem to expand their enterprises and sell their $175,000 wares to billionaire collectors.
