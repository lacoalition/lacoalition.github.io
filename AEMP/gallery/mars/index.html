Museum as Retail Space (MaRS)

Museum as Retail Space (MaRS) was operated by Robert Zin Stark out of a building his mother purchased on his behalf for $700,000. Stark himself lives in a million-dollar condo loft in the Downtown Arts District.)

 

MaRS is located in a part of Boyle Heights near the LA River where longstanding public housing abuts industrial businesses and factories. At the gallery’s opening, art collectors gazed at $10,000 paintings while working-class residents were struggling to afford rent increases not far from the gallery’s location. Buyers took $50 Uber rides to visit the luxury art outpost in an area with a median household income is $21,700. It was a clear image of income inequality and what gentrifiers really value (capital) in Los Angeles. Who could seriously argue that spaces like this benefit poor communities? No local street vendors or long-time community members were likely to frequent MaRS. A real community benefit would be laundromats, childcare centers, and additional services of the like. The community decided to amplify their dislike and anger.

 

BHAAAD and other local organizers initiated picket lines outside MaRS in May 2017. These actions became a popular and effective tool to dissuade would-be patrons from breaking the boycott against gentrifying spaces in Boyle Heights. More than once, Stark attempted to co-opt protests by claiming he was sympathetic to community needs. Other times, he took a more dangerous approach by calling the LAPD to harass protestors. When poor people of color are subject to state-sanctioned violence, the fact that this wealthy white gentrifier chose to call the cops—because we had a simple picket line—illustrates how little he cares for Boyle Heights residents and their safety. 

 

MaRS closed down in May 2018 after strong criticism and organizing. Stark claimed to understand what he did wrong by opening a gallery in Boyle Heights, and even met with members of various anti-gentrification groups to address how he could be held accountable and remedy some impacts of his actions. But as of today, he has dropped any effort to convert the space for community needs and has opted to turn the former gallery space into a cannabis production/manufacturing shop.

 

Today, additional galleries, as well as tech spaces and coffee shops, surround what is left of MaRS’s physical space. Their quick proliferation demonstrates how art spaces catering to the upper echelons of society attract other businesses designed to those with higher incomes.
